Neutral question answered:In general terms, a coordination problem is "hard" for any group of independent agents (whether humans, animals, robots, or software processes) trying to act together without a central controller when three core difficulties collide:Misaligned incentives or goals: Each agent has its own private objectives, information, or payoffs. What looks optimal for one agent can actively harm the group outcome (classic examples include the Prisoner's Dilemma or tragedy of the commons). Without a trusted way to enforce cooperation, rational self-interest can lead everyone to worse collective results.
Incomplete or asymmetric information: Agents don't share perfect knowledge of each other's intentions, capabilities, states, or the environment. This creates uncertainty — should I trust you to do your part? Will you still cooperate if I do? Communication channels are costly, noisy, or untrustworthy, so agents must guess or hedge, often leading to inefficiency, defection, or deadlock.
Lack of enforceable commitment mechanisms: Without a central authority to monitor, punish defection, or bind future actions, promises are cheap and hard to verify. Agents must rely on reputation, repeated interaction, signaling, or emergent norms — all of which can fail under noise, changing conditions, or temptation. Scaling this to larger groups makes it exponentially harder because monitoring costs rise and free-riding becomes easier to hide.

The hardness comes from the combinatorial explosion: each agent must model not only the environment but also the likely models and actions of every other agent (and their models of others, and so on). Small uncertainties compound into instability, and simple rule-based solutions often collapse under real-world friction, timing mismatches, or adversarial behavior.
